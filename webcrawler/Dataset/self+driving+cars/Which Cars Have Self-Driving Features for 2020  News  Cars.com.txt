                    <p><span style="font-weight: 400;">The good news? Some <b style="color:#000;background:#ffff66">self</b>-<b style="color:#000;background:#66ffff">driving</b> features can help ease the burden of your commute or road trip. No current production car in the U.S. can drive while you sleep, read or tweet, but many systems can maintain following distance with the vehicle ahead or keep your car centered in its lane — even down to a stop in bumper-to-bumper traffic, and in some cases without your holding the wheel.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Such capabilities have proliferated. When we began documenting <b style="color:#000;background:#ffff66">self</b>-<b style="color:#000;background:#66ffff">driving</b> features across the industry </span><a href="https://www.cars.com/articles/self-driving-cars-the-big-list-of-which-automakers-do-what-1420684684889/"><span style="font-weight: 400;">in 2016</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;">, only a handful of vehicles had lane-centering steering, and only some of those — mostly from Mercedes-Benz — did so from stop-and-go traffic up to highway speeds. Today, scores of models offer such full-range functionality, some as affordable as a base-model Toyota Corolla. A few BMWs and one Cadillac will even steer, accelerate and brake without your having to hold the wheel (provided a driver-facing camera intuits you’re paying attention).</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Which <b style="color:#000;background:#ffcc99">cars</b> have what? We simplified this list for 2020 to include the root nameplates only, so bear in mind that powertrain or body style variants — such as extended-length versions, or high-performance or hybrid variants — may also include the listed feature. In some cases, we singled out certain variants if the feature</span> <span style="font-weight: 400;">is </span><i><span style="font-weight: 400;">exclusive</span></i><span style="font-weight: 400;"> to them: For example, only the all-electric Kia Niro EV, not its gasoline counterpart, has lane-centering steering. But for the most part, we kept things at a high level.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Alphabetically by mass-market brand, here’s is the lay of the <b style="color:#000;background:#ffff66">self</b>-<b style="color:#000;background:#66ffff">driving</b> land.</span></p>

<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Volvo’s Pilot Assist system, which incorporates lane-centering steering and adaptive cruise control all the way to a stop.</span></p>
<p><a href="https://www.cars.com/research/volvo/"><b>Research Volvo Vehicles</b></a></p>

<ul>
<li style="font-weight: 400;"><a href="https://www.cars.com/articles/which-cars-have-self-driving-features-for-2018-1420699785509/"><span style="font-weight: 400;">Which <b style="color:#000;background:#ffcc99">Cars</b> Have <b style="color:#000;background:#ffff66">Self</b>-<b style="color:#000;background:#66ffff">Driving</b> Features for 2018?</span></a></li>

</ul>
<h2><b>More About the Systems</b></h2>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Myriad differences exist in the systems listed above, and performance varies widely. In our evaluations, some systems maintain fastidious lane centering and respond smoothly to changing traffic conditions. Others can allow significant wandering in your lane, react suddenly to changing traffic patterns or both. Varying conditions, from weather to road markings, can affect whether — and how well — each system works. It’s a good idea to download the owner’s manual of your prospective car to learn how its <b style="color:#000;background:#ffff66">self</b>-<b style="color:#000;background:#66ffff">driving</b> systems work, and ask a dealer specialist for a tutorial when you buy the car. And, of course, pay attention to the road.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Want to know more? Read on.</span></p>
<h3>Adaptive Cruise Control Down to a Stop</h3>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">This feature builds on basic adaptive cruise control, a </span><a href="http://wardsauto.com/news-analysis/smart-cruisemercedes-first-adaptive-cruise-control-system"><span style="font-weight: 400;">decades-old feature</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;"> that maintains a selectable distance between you and the car ahead. Adaptive cruise that works at </span><i><span style="font-weight: 400;">higher</span></i><span style="font-weight: 400;"> speeds is widely available, but systems that function down to a full standstill are an important next step toward managing bumper-to-bumper traffic. Such systems can bring you to a full stop in traffic. Some require you to apply the brakes and reaccelerate afterward, while others can resume speed when the car ahead moves within a given timeframe.</span></p>
<h3>Lane-Centering Steering</h3>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">This goes beyond lane-departure steering assist, which intervenes only as you approach or cross the lane markings — and often pinballs you back toward the </span><i><span style="font-weight: 400;">opposite</span></i><span style="font-weight: 400;"> markings — to actively center the vehicle in its lane by tracking lane markings, the vehicle ahead or some combination of the two. Such systems can often negotiate mild curves, as well, but nearly all of them require you to keep your hands on the wheel, issuing warnings and eventually deactivating if they sense a lack of steering force after a short time. Many lane-centering steering systems will also deactivate if lane markings disappear, which often occurs with construction zones, merging lanes or poorly marked roads. Some systems operate only above or below a certain speed threshold, but most modern systems now function from a stop all the way up to highway speeds.</span></p>
<h3>Hands-Free Steering</h3>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">This centers the car </span><i><span style="font-weight: 400;">without </span></i><span style="font-weight: 400;">your hands on the wheel. For 2020, only two systems — Cadillac’s Super Cruise and BMW’s Extended Traffic Jam Assistant — do this. Both require you to pay attention, intuited via driver-facing cameras.</span></p>
<h3>Attention-Free <b style="color:#000;background:#66ffff">Driving</b></h3>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Conditional automated <b style="color:#000;background:#66ffff">driving</b>, where your car putters along in specific situations and you </span><i><span style="font-weight: 400;">don’t </span></i><span style="font-weight: 400;">have to pay attention, was to come with Audi’s new </span><a href="https://www.cars.com/articles/2019-audi-a8-has-high-self-driving-capability-are-we-ready-1420698230637/"><span style="font-weight: 400;">Traffic Jam Pilot</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;">, a feature made possible in low-speed traffic through some two dozen camera, radar, sonic and laser sensors on the brand’s flagship A8 sedan. (You’d have to be able to take over if the system determined that conditions required it, so you couldn’t, for example, take a nap.) But regulatory challenges </span><a href="https://www.cars.com/articles/heres-the-rub-new-audi-a8-can-massage-your-feet-but-not-drive-itself-1420700846380/"><span style="font-weight: 400;">stymied the technology</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;"> for the U.S. market, so, as of now, the A8 — or any other Audi in the U.S., for that matter — does not offer TJP. As of yet, no production car in the U.S. has advanced to this point.</span></p>
